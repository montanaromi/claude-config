# Blitzy Blog Style Guide

## Blog Series

Each series has a distinct tone. Adjust validation weight accordingly.

| Series | Tone | Validation emphasis |
|--------|------|---------------------|
| **Blitzy Open Source Enhancement Initiative** | Technically rigorous, development-focused | Weight principles 3 (simplicity) and 6 (say what you mean) highest. Technical depth expected. Jargon acceptable when precise. |
| **Building with Blitzy** | Relatable, accessible, cross-functional audience | Weight principles 2 (don't ramble), 5 (sound like yourself), 7 (pity the reader) highest. Minimize jargon. Explain technical concepts. |
| **The Blitzy Log** | Forward-looking, showcase cutting-edge use cases | Weight principles 1 (care about it) and 8 (start near the end) highest. Lead with the novel angle. |

If the series is unknown, ask: "Which series is this for? (Open Source Enhancement / Building with Blitzy / The Blitzy Log)"

## Blitzy Blog Rules

These rules layer ON TOP of Vonnegut's 8 principles. When `--blog` is active, validate all 8 Vonnegut principles PLUS these 5 rules.

### B1: No Em Dashes

Em dashes have become a hallmark of AI-generated writing. The Blitzy Blog must not read like AI wrote it.

**Detection:** Scan for `—` (U+2014) and `--` used as em dashes.

**Remediation:** Replace with commas, colons, parentheses, or split into two sentences. Choose whichever punctuation fits the point being made.

**Examples:**
- Before: "Blitzy handles the heavy lifting — from code generation to testing."
- After: "Blitzy handles the heavy lifting, from code generation to testing."
- Before: "The platform — which supports 40+ languages — processed the entire codebase."
- After: "The platform supports 40+ languages. It processed the entire codebase."

### B2: No "It" or "This" as Sentence Starters

Starting a sentence with "it" or "this" as the subject lacks the precision the blog demands. Scientific and technical writing is clearer when subjects are named explicitly.

**Detection:** Sentences beginning with "It " or "This " where the word serves as the grammatical subject (not part of a clause like "It is worth noting that" rewritten as a direct statement).

**Remediation:** Replace "it" or "this" with the specific noun or concept being referenced. Rework the sentence if needed.

**Examples:**
- Before: "This increases deployment speed by 40%."
- After: "Automated testing increases deployment speed by 40%."
- Before: "It processes codebases up to 100M lines."
- After: "Blitzy processes codebases up to 100M lines."

### B3: No Passive Voice

Active voice engages the reader's brain differently than passive voice. Neurolinguistic research shows active constructions activate motor and sensory cortices more strongly, making the reader feel closer to the action. Passive voice distances the reader from the subject and dulls the writing.

**Active voice:** The subject performs the action. "Blitzy generated 3,000 lines of code."
**Passive voice:** The subject receives the action. "3,000 lines of code were generated by Blitzy."

**Detection:** Look for forms of "to be" + past participle: "was generated", "is handled", "were processed", "has been deployed", "are configured".

**Remediation:** Identify who or what performs the action. Make that the subject.

**Examples:**
- Before: "The codebase was analyzed by three specialized agents."
- After: "Three specialized agents analyzed the codebase."
- Before: "Errors are caught during the validation phase."
- After: "The validation phase catches errors."

**The exception:** Passive voice is appropriate when the actor is genuinely unknown, irrelevant, or when the object of the action is the real focus:
- "The vulnerability was discovered in 2024." (who found it is irrelevant to the point)
- "Python was released in 1991." (historical fact, actor obvious)
- "The server was decommissioned." (emphasis on the server, not who did it)

When passive voice is acceptable, do not flag it. Note it as a deliberate choice.

### B4: No Word/Phrase Repetition

Repeating the same word or phrase throughout a piece makes the writing feel mechanical. Variety keeps readers engaged.

**Detection:** Flag any non-technical word or phrase that appears 3+ times within 500 words (excluding articles, prepositions, and proper nouns like "Blitzy").

**Remediation:**
- For non-technical terms: use a synonym. Consult a thesaurus (OED, Merriam-Webster).
- For technical terms: use the Blitzy Dictionary (see below) for approved synonyms.

**Blitzy Dictionary — Approved Technical Synonyms:**

| Term | Acceptable synonyms |
|------|---------------------|
| coding | programming, development, implementation |
| codebase | repository, code, project |
| agent | specialist, worker (when context is clear) |
| generate | produce, create, build, write |
| deploy | ship, release, launch |
| bug | defect, issue, error |
| test | validate, verify, check |
| prompt | instruction, directive, specification |
| AI | artificial intelligence, machine intelligence (first use only, then AI) |

Add to this dictionary as the blog evolves.

### B5: Cite Your Sources

Every claim, statistic, or external reference must include a hyperlink to the source.

**Detection:** Look for:
- Statistics without links ("40% faster", "3x improvement")
- Named projects, tools, or libraries without links
- Claims about industry trends without attribution

**Remediation:** Add a hyperlink. If no source exists, flag for the author: "This claim needs a source: [quote]. Add a link or remove the statistic."
